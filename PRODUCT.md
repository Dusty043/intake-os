# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Internal operations team at Simple.biz — project managers, DevOps leads, intake owners, and approvers. They use this tool as part of their actual workflow, not occasionally. They are technical-adjacent and expect software that respects their intelligence. Primary task on any given screen: review and move a project request through the pipeline.

## Product Purpose

Project Intake OS is the internal control layer for all new project work. It captures project requests, runs them through an AI-guided discovery conversation and multi-dimension evaluation, routes them through approval gates, and distributes approved projects to Monday.com and GitHub. It exists to replace ad-hoc Slack requests and unclear handoffs with a traceable, governed process. Success means every project that reaches a developer has been properly scoped, evaluated, and approved.

## Brand Personality

Confident, precise, earned.

This is serious internal infrastructure. It should feel like the team built something they're proud of — not a third-party SaaS they're tolerating. The UI earns respect rather than begs for attention.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS**: The current state. Indigo-600 buttons, rounded-xl cards, gray-50 table rows, Inter on a near-white canvas. Looks like every Tailwind starter. Nothing about it says "we built this."
- **Enterprise bloatware**: Gray-on-gray, nested accordions, status modals that open modals. Heavy, slow, visually fatiguing. No personality.
- **Startup playfulness**: Consumer app energy — illustrations, bouncy animations, bright illustrations. This is operational tooling.
- **Minimal to empty**: So much whitespace it looks undesigned. Hierarchy without substance.

## Design Principles

1. **Density with clarity.** Information-dense like a professional tool, but hierarchy is unambiguous. Every element has a visual role.
2. **Dark by intent, not by trend.** A dark shell isn't a style choice — it reduces eye fatigue during extended use and signals "this is a working environment."
3. **No decoration without purpose.** Color, shadow, and motion serve navigation and state — not aesthetics for their own sake.
4. **States are first-class citizens.** Empty states, loading states, error states, and review states are designed, not fallback-rendered.
5. **Actions are obvious.** The primary action on any screen is never ambiguous. Secondary actions recede.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Text contrast ≥4.5:1 on all surfaces. Focus states visible and keyboard-navigable throughout. Reduced-motion alternatives for any animation.
